01 Framing the Problem

5W1H: Defining the design challenge

Who

People who lose personal belongings in public spaces, transit, and events, plus finders who want to return items without hassle

What

A mobile platform that connects lost items with their owners through AI matching, ownership verification, and coordinated return

When

Immediately after losing or finding an item, when urgency is highest and existing systems fail to provide any clear next step

Where

Public transit, airports, events, cafes, and shared spaces across European cities, starting with Berlin

Why

85% of lost items are never returned. Lost-and-found offices operate in silos. Finders have no clear way to return items. No cross-organization protocol exists

How

Camera-first item reporting, AI photo matching, multi-step ownership verification, safe meetup coordination, and shipping integration

02 Discovery and Research

6 weeks of deep research

I spent 6 weeks in discovery before designing a single screen. I interviewed people who lost items, people who found items, and lost-and-found office operators. I mapped the regulatory landscape across Germany and the EU to understand legal requirements around found property.

User interviews (n=16)

Spoke with 8 "losers" (people who lost items), 4 "finders," and 4 lost-and-found operators across Berlin, Munich, and Amsterdam. Key finding: the emotional distress of losing items is severely underestimated. It is not just about the object's monetary value.

Competitive analysis

Analyzed 8 solutions (iLost, Tile, Foundit, BVG Fundburo). Most were device-only (Tile) or single-organization (iLost). None offered a true cross-organization protocol. This validated our infrastructure-first approach.

Regulatory mapping

German law (BGB sections 965-984) defines legal obligations for finders. EU GDPR adds complexity around sharing personal data between parties. These constraints directly shaped the ownership verification and data-handling flows.

Service blueprint

Created an end-to-end blueprint mapping frontstage interactions, backstage processes, and support systems. This revealed the 7 critical handoff points where items typically get lost in the current system.

05 Final Designs

Onboarding and item reporting

The onboarding communicates three value props through illustrated screens: report, match, return. Sign-up uses email plus OTP for simplicity, with Google SSO as an alternative. Item reporting is camera-first: photograph the item, add details, pin the location, and post.

Matching, verification, and communication

When the AI finds a potential match, both parties see a match confidence score (e.g., 98%). The finder reviews ownership proof (serial numbers, photos, purchase receipts) before approving. In-app messaging coordinates the return without sharing personal contact details.

Ownership verification and return coordination

Users provide proof of ownership through serial numbers, purchase receipts, photographs, or warranty documents. The system supports both in-person meetups (with safe location suggestions and a handover code) and shipping through integrated delivery partners with real-time tracking.

Safe meetups and claim resolution

The in-person meetup flow suggests safe public locations (police stations, cafes, libraries), handles time scheduling, and uses a 4-digit handover code for identity verification. For disputed claims, the reject flow communicates decisions respectfully while protecting both parties.

Complete screen architecture

The full app spans 47 screens across onboarding, item reporting, AI matching, ownership verification, messaging, delivery coordination, and rating. Every screen was designed mobile-first with accessibility built in from the start.

Meeting location suggestions needed context

Users loved safe meeting spots but wanted to know why they were "safe." Added rationale labels: "Police station: public, CCTV, staff present."

10/12 testers said the "98% match" indicator gave them confidence the system was working. Without it, users questioned whether the match was real.

Wrong code error was unclear. Added: "Code does not match. Ask the other person to show their code screen" with retry.

07 Reflections

What I learned

Designing for trust is the hardest problem

In a platform where strangers exchange personal belongings, every interaction must build trust. Trust is not a feature. It emerges from dozens of small decisions: clear language, verification steps, safety features, and transparent processes.

0 to 1 requires ruthless prioritization

With limited resources, I developed the framework "What is the minimum viable trust?" The smallest set of features that makes someone comfortable handing belongings to a stranger through the platform.

The design system scales the product

The component library I built supports the mobile app, a B2B admin dashboard, and API documentation from the same token set. New flows can be assembled in hours, not weeks.

Regulatory constraints improve design

German found-property law and GDPR initially felt like obstacles. They turned out to be forcing functions that made the verification flow more trustworthy. Legal requirements and good UX aligned perfectly.
